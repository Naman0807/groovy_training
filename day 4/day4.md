# My First 4 Days at Groovy Web: Scaling Intent

The transition from "typing" to "prompting" this week at Groovy Web was not just a change in tool usage, but a complete mental refactor.

### Beyond the Basics
This week wasn't just about installing VS Code or generating API keys. It was about understanding the **mechanics of AI-IDE workflows**. By installing Continue.dev and configuring it to work alongside Claude Projects, I transitioned from a "reactive" coder to a "proactive" architect. I learned that the bottleneck in modern engineering is rarely the writing of syntax, but the management of context—specifically how to feed the right files and documentation into an LLM to get an architectural-grade response.

### The Multi-LLM Insight
My biggest surprise was how much model selection matters based on the *intent* of the task. I tested five core prompts—ranging from React lifecycle analysis to complex FastAPI security configurations—and found that Claude 3.5 Sonnet excels at refactoring and human-readable code, whereas ChatGPT and Gemini provide deeper structural reasoning for complex systems. This isn't just theory; it’s now part of my development lifecycle where I route different types of tasks to the model that handles them most efficiently.

### Engineering for the Future
Setting up my personal `prompt-library.md` and defining role-based Claude Projects (Frontend, Backend, and Code Reviewer) has effectively created a "senior developer" mirror that I can query at any time. As I head into Week 2, I am no longer just looking to generate code, but to engineer the *systems* that generate, test, and deploy that code.
